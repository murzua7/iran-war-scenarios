1. Executive Summary and Policy Implications

Key Judgements

The de facto closure of the Strait of Hormuz on March 2, 2026, following the launch of Operation Epic Fury, constitutes the most severe disruption to global energy supply since the formation of OPEC. Approximately 20 million barrels per day of crude oil transit — representing 20 per cent of global seaborne oil trade — has been reduced by an estimated 70 per cent. Simultaneously, Qatar's LNG production, accounting for roughly 20 per cent of global supply, has been suspended and partially destroyed. The International Energy Agency declared this the largest oil supply disruption in recorded history on March 13. The confluence of an oil transit disruption with physical destruction of LNG export infrastructure is unprecedented in scale and represents a structural, not merely transitory, supply shock.

The current crisis differs qualitatively from previous energy disruptions — including the 1973 Arab oil embargo, the 1979 Iranian Revolution, the 1990 Gulf War, and the 2022 Russia-Ukraine energy shock — in three material respects. First, the disruption affects both crude oil transit and liquefied natural gas simultaneously, through the same chokepoint and the same conflict. Second, the physical destruction of Ras Laffan, Qatar's primary LNG export facility, introduces a multi-year structural deficit that will persist well beyond any ceasefire. The QatarEnergy CEO has stated that 17 per cent of export capacity was destroyed in the March 18–19 strikes, with full reconstruction requiring up to five years. Third, the conflict is actively escalating, with energy infrastructure on both belligerent and neutral Gulf states now being deliberately targeted, removing the historical convention of energy assets as de facto protected infrastructure.

The macroeconomic implications are severe. Brent crude has risen from approximately $71 per barrel in late February to a peak of $126 on March 8, settling around $101–103 in recent trading. Diesel and heating oil have experienced even sharper increases — 47 per cent and 81 per cent respectively — indicating acute refining and distillate supply stress beyond the crude price shock alone. The coordinated release of 400 million barrels from IEA member strategic reserves provides approximately 40–50 days of coverage at current disruption rates, but this is a finite buffer that cannot substitute for restored transit. Central banks face a familiar but acute dilemma: an exogenous supply shock that raises both inflation expectations and recession risk simultaneously.

Critical Assessment

At current conflict trajectories, the global economy faces a sustained energy supply shortfall of 8–10 million barrels per day of crude oil and the loss of approximately 80 million tonnes per annum of LNG capacity. Existing strategic reserves and emergency releases provide a temporary buffer of 40–50 days. Should the Strait remain effectively closed beyond June 2026, rationing of petroleum products in import-dependent economies (India, Pakistan, Bangladesh) becomes probable, and a global recession triggered by energy cost pass-through becomes the central scenario.

2. Conflict Overview and Energy Infrastructure Damage

Operation Epic Fury commenced on February 28, 2026, with coordinated US-Israeli airstrikes on Iranian military facilities, nuclear sites, and leadership targets, including a decapitation strike that killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei. The conflict entered its twentieth day on March 19. The initial phase (February 28–March 7) focused on military and nuclear targets. The second phase (March 8 onwards) has been marked by an escalating cycle of strikes on energy infrastructure, a development that has dramatically amplified the economic consequences of the conflict.

The Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps announced the closure of the Strait of Hormuz on March 2 via VHF radio broadcast. Notably, Iran did not declare a formal legal blockade under international law. Instead, the de facto closure was achieved through a combination of anti-ship cruise missiles, armed drones, GPS jamming, and the withdrawal of commercial maritime insurance coverage — which rendered transit commercially unviable regardless of physical risk tolerance. Ship-tracking data confirmed a 70 per cent reduction in traffic within 48 hours. Iran subsequently adopted a selective passage policy, permitting transit by Indian, Pakistani, and certain neutral-flagged vessels that dock at Iranian ports, while maintaining interdiction of US-allied commercial shipping.

3. Crude Oil Markets: Price Dynamics and Supply Shortfall

Crude oil markets have experienced the most rapid sustained price increase since the outbreak of the Russia-Ukraine conflict in February 2022, though the current shock is larger in magnitude and structural duration. Brent crude rose from $71.32 on February 27 to a peak of $126 on March 8 — a 77 per cent increase in nine trading sessions. Following the IEA's announcement of a 400 million barrel coordinated release on March 11, prices retreated to the $101–103 range, where they have traded with elevated intraday volatility (3–5 per cent daily ranges). WTI followed a similar trajectory, rising from $66.96 to $98.48, though the Brent–WTI spread has widened to approximately $7.65, up from a pre-crisis spread of roughly $5 — reflecting tighter international supply conditions relative to the US domestic market, which benefits from shale production and pipeline-connected Canadian imports.

The scale of the supply disruption is historically unprecedented. Approximately 20 million barrels per day of crude oil and condensate normally transits the Strait of Hormuz, representing roughly 20 per cent of global seaborne oil trade. With traffic reduced by an estimated 70 per cent, the net shortfall is approximately 14 mb/d of transit capacity, though not all of this volume is immediately lost — some Gulf producers can reroute limited quantities via the East-West Pipeline (capacity: 5 mb/d, current utilisation: approximately 2 mb/d) and the Abu Dhabi Crude Oil Pipeline (ADCOP, capacity: 1.5 mb/d). The IEA estimates the net global supply shortfall at 8–10 mb/d after accounting for alternative routing and production curtailments by Gulf states under force majeure.

Supply Shortfall Assessment

The net disruption of 8–10 mb/d exceeds any previous supply shock. For comparison: the 1990 Gulf War removed approximately 4.3 mb/d; the 1979 Iranian Revolution disrupted approximately 5.6 mb/d; the 2019 Abqaiq–Khurais attack temporarily removed 5.7 mb/d (restored within weeks). The current disruption is both larger in magnitude and longer in expected duration. OPEC+ has offered to increase output by 206,000 b/d — a token 2 per cent offset that underscores the cartel's limited spare capacity.

4. Refined Products: Downstream Pass-Through and Consumer Impact

Retail fuel prices have responded aggressively, with distillates (diesel and heating oil) exhibiting considerably sharper increases than gasoline — a pattern consistent with refinery-specific supply constraints compounding the crude oil shock. US retail diesel has risen from $3.46 per gallon in mid-January to $5.07 as of the week ending March 16, a 47 per cent increase and the first breach of the $5 level since the 2022 energy crisis. Heating oil at the New York Harbor benchmark has surged 81 per cent from $1.99 to $3.59 over the same period, the most extreme move among all petroleum products tracked.

Figure 3. US weekly retail gasoline and diesel prices, December 2025–March 2026. Diesel's disproportionate increase (+47% vs. +34% for gasoline) signals distillate-specific supply stress. Source: FRED (GASREGW, GASDESW).
Figure 4. New York Harbor heating oil price, January–March 2026. The 81% increase represents the sharpest move in any major petroleum product. Source: FRED (WHOILNYH).

The disproportionate impact on distillates carries significant macroeconomic implications. Diesel is the primary fuel for freight transport, agricultural equipment, and industrial heating. A sustained $5+ diesel price increases marginal shipping costs by approximately 15–20 per cent for long-haul trucking, with pass-through to consumer goods prices within 4–8 weeks. Heating oil, though less systemically important than diesel in the US, is the primary heating fuel for approximately 5.3 million households concentrated in the Northeast — a politically sensitive constituency entering the spring heating season's tail end.

US regular gasoline has risen from $2.78 per gallon to $3.72 — a 34 per cent increase that, while less dramatic than distillates, is nonetheless the fastest sustained gasoline price rally since the March 2022 Russia-Ukraine shock. The typical two-week lag between crude oil and pump prices suggests further increases are embedded, with multiple analysts projecting $4.00–$4.50 per gallon by April if Brent remains above $100.

5. Natural Gas and LNG: The Qatar Structural Shock

The natural gas market presents a bifurcated picture. US domestic natural gas (Henry Hub) has remained remarkably stable at approximately $3.03/MMBtu, reflecting the US's position as a net natural gas exporter with ample domestic shale production. The January 2026 spike to $7.72 (with a single-day peak of $30.72) was driven by an Arctic cold-weather event unrelated to the Middle East conflict. However, international LNG markets tell a fundamentally different story.

Figure 5. Henry Hub natural gas prices (monthly average, $/MMBtu), March 2024–February 2026. Domestic US gas prices have recovered from historic lows but remain moderate, insulated by domestic production. Source: Alpha Vantage.
Figure 6. LNG spot prices, Asia and Europe (monthly, $/MMBtu). Note the convergence in late 2025 as both markets softened, now diverging as competition for non-Qatari supply intensifies. February–March 2026 data not yet available at monthly frequency; spot reports indicate European prices surging 35% on March 19 alone. Source: FRED (PNGASJPUSDM, PNGASEUUSDM).

Qatar is the world's second-largest LNG exporter, producing approximately 80 million tonnes per annum from its North Field facilities at Ras Laffan Industrial City. QatarEnergy suspended all production on March 2 following Iranian drone attacks and declared force majeure on all long-term LNG contracts on March 4. The March 18–19 coordinated strikes inflicted what QatarEnergy's CEO described as "extensive damage" to primary liquefaction trains, with 17 per cent of total capacity physically destroyed. This transforms the disruption from a temporary transit issue into a structural LNG supply deficit with a multi-year recovery horizon.

European gas prices surged 35 per cent on March 19 following the Ras Laffan strikes, with TTF futures breaching levels not seen since the 2022 Russian gas crisis. Europe is particularly exposed: having pivoted aggressively away from Russian pipeline gas following the 2022 invasion of Ukraine, European buyers increased their dependence on Qatari LNG imports from approximately 5 per cent to 12–14 per cent of total LNG supply. The loss of both Russian pipeline gas and Qatari LNG represents a compounding of supply shocks that the European gas market was not designed to absorb.

At least nine LNG cargoes originally contracted for European delivery have been diverted to Asian buyers willing to pay spot premiums, a pattern consistent with the 2021–2022 LNG diversion dynamics but now operating under structural rather than cyclical scarcity. South Asian countries — Pakistan (99 per cent of LNG imports from the Gulf), Bangladesh (72 per cent), and India (53 per cent) — face acute LNG shortages, with Pakistan already implementing industrial rationing.

Structural LNG Deficit

The physical destruction of Ras Laffan capacity (17 per cent, multi-year repair) means that even an immediate ceasefire would not restore Qatari LNG supply to pre-crisis levels for years. Global LNG markets will operate under structural deficit conditions through at least 2028, with particularly severe consequences for import-dependent economies in South and Southeast Asia, and for European winter gas procurement.

6. Strategic Reserves and Emergency Coordination

On March 11, the International Energy Agency's 32 member countries unanimously agreed to release 400 million barrels of crude oil and petroleum products from strategic reserves — the largest coordinated release in the IEA's history and only the sixth collective action since the agency's founding in 1974. For comparison, the previous record was the 2022 coordinated release of approximately 240 million barrels in response to the Russia-Ukraine conflict. The scale of the current release reflects the severity of the disruption.

The arithmetic of the release is sobering. At a sustained supply shortfall of 8–10 mb/d, 400 million barrels provides approximately 40–50 days of coverage. Should the Strait remain closed through mid-May, the coordinated release will be substantially depleted, and the US SPR — at approximately 243 million barrels post-release — will have only 34 per cent of its 714 million barrel capacity remaining, the lowest level since the reserve's establishment in 1975. This constrains the credibility of future release announcements as a market stabilisation tool.

7. Country-Level Vulnerability Assessment

The distributional impact of the Strait of Hormuz closure is highly asymmetric. Countries with large domestic production (the United States, Canada, Brazil, Norway) face price shocks but not physical supply constraints. Import-dependent economies with substantial Hormuz exposure face both. Asia receives 89.2 per cent of all crude oil and condensate transiting the Strait, making this fundamentally an Asian energy security crisis with global price ramifications.

Japan and South Korea represent the positive end of the preparedness spectrum. Japan maintains 230–254 days of total reserve cover (146 days government strategic, 101–108 days commercial) — the highest among major industrialised nations. Japan has already begun releasing 80 million barrels under the IEA coordination, partly by lowering the mandatory private stockpile requirement from 70 to 55 days. South Korea maintains comparable cover at 200–210 days. Both countries, however, are heavily exposed to a prolonged closure: approximately 80 per cent of Japan's crude imports and 68 per cent of South Korea's transit through Hormuz. Their reserves provide a buffer of 7–9 months against complete Hormuz dependence, after which alternative sourcing (West Africa, Americas) at higher cost and longer transit times becomes essential.

India represents the most critical vulnerability among major economies. Its strategic petroleum reserve covers only approximately 7 days of consumption — an order of magnitude below the IEA's 90-day benchmark. Combined with commercial stocks, total reserve cover is approximately 72 days. India imports roughly 50 per cent of its crude through Hormuz and 53 per cent of its LNG from the Gulf. India has already begun rationing LPG for industrial use to protect household supplies, and the government has been negotiating directly with Iran for selective transit passage — partially successfully, with Indian LPG tankers receiving passage permits.

China's reserve position is opaque but substantial. Satellite imagery analysis by Kpler and Ursa Space estimates government SPR at 400–500 million barrels in above-ground storage (Phases I–III), with total reserves including commercial stocks at 1.2–1.3 billion barrels. China has been aggressively building reserves since 2024, with 11 new storage sites adding approximately 169 million barrels of capacity. China's Hormuz dependence is approximately 40 per cent, lower than East Asian peers but still significant given the absolute volume (approximately 4.5 mb/d of Gulf crude). China's selective diplomatic positioning — maintaining communication with Iran while not opposing US operations — provides it with unique leverage in securing alternative transit arrangements.

8. European Energy Security: Gas Storage and Winter Risk

European gas storage entered the 2025/26 winter season at approximately 63 per cent capacity — below the prior year's level and below the EU's target trajectory. The draw-down has been heavy, and as of mid-March 2026, aggregate EU gas storage stands at approximately 26–30 per cent — the lowest seasonal level in recent years. Individual country fill rates vary: Germany at 30.2 per cent, France at 29 per cent, the Netherlands at 23.5 per cent, Italy at approximately 35 per cent, and Spain at approximately 45 per cent (benefiting from diversified LNG import infrastructure and North African pipeline connections).

Figure 10. EU gas storage fill rates by country (mid-March 2026) versus the 90% November target mandated by EU regulation 2022/1032. The gap between current levels and the target — approximately 60 percentage points — must be filled during the April–October injection season, now severely complicated by the loss of Qatari LNG. Source: Gas Infrastructure Europe (GIE), EU Council.

The EU must inject at least 575 TWh of natural gas during the April–October 2026 injection season to meet the legally mandated 90 per cent fill target by November. Under normal supply conditions, this is achievable but tight. With Qatari LNG offline (12–14 per cent of EU LNG imports) and spot LNG prices elevated due to Asian competition, the procurement challenge is acute. The EU has limited near-term alternatives: US LNG exports are already at capacity, Australian LNG is primarily contracted to Asian buyers, and Norwegian pipeline gas is at maximum throughput. The structural loss of Ras Laffan capacity (multi-year repair) means this is not a one-winter problem but a sustained supply constraint through at least 2028–2029.

European Winter 2026/27 Risk

Should Qatari LNG remain offline and spot LNG prices sustain above $15/MMBtu, the EU faces a material risk of failing the 90% storage fill target. This would replicate the 2022 dynamic of demand rationing, industrial curtailment, and high consumer prices — but from a weaker starting position (lower storage, less Russian gas substitution remaining). Germany, France, and the Netherlands are most exposed.

9. Secondary Energy Markets: Coal and Electricity

Coal and electricity prices have thus far remained remarkably stable, indicating that the current shock is concentrated in oil-derived products and LNG rather than representing a broad-based energy crisis — at least at this stage. Australian thermal coal trades at approximately $115.82/MT, essentially unchanged from late 2025 levels and well below the $150+ levels of late 2024. US average electricity prices remain at $0.189/kWh, reflecting the US generation mix's relatively low oil dependence (approximately 0.5 per cent of US electricity comes from petroleum).

However, this stability is unlikely to persist if the crisis extends. European electricity prices are indexed to marginal gas generation costs; as TTF gas prices rise, electricity prices follow with near-unit elasticity. Asian economies that rely on oil-fired generation (notably Japan, where approximately 6 per cent of generation is oil-fired) will see electricity cost pass-through. Coal prices may also rise as gas-to-coal switching accelerates in Europe and Asia, tightening the seaborne thermal coal market.

10. Macroeconomic and Monetary Policy Implications

The energy supply shock presents central banks with the classical supply-side dilemma: a simultaneous increase in inflation (via energy cost pass-through) and decrease in output (via terms-of-trade deterioration, reduced consumer purchasing power, and disrupted supply chains). The policy response is inherently constrained — monetary tightening can anchor inflation expectations but deepens the output contraction; accommodation prevents recession but risks de-anchoring inflation expectations that were only recently re-anchored following the 2021–2023 inflation episode.

Inflation Pass-Through Channels

The primary inflation transmission mechanisms are: (i) direct energy cost pass-through to CPI (gasoline, heating, electricity); (ii) indirect cost-push via diesel and freight costs (food, consumer goods, industrial inputs); and (iii) second-round effects via wage demands if inflation expectations become unanchored. Monetary Policy Assessment

We recommend maintaining the current policy rate at this juncture, with forward guidance signalling that the Committee views the energy shock as primarily supply-driven and will "look through" the first-round inflation effects provided that medium-term inflation expectations remain anchored. However, should 5-year breakeven inflation rates rise above [threshold], a pre-emptive tightening response would be warranted. The risk of premature tightening — deepening a supply-shock recession — currently exceeds the risk of delayed tightening, given the transitory nature of the shock under our base scenario.

Our central scenario (B, 45 per cent probability) assumes the conflict persists through Q3 2026 with gradual, partial reopening of the Strait by August following diplomatic or military de-escalation. Under this scenario, Brent averages $110–140 per barrel for Q2–Q3, the IEA coordinated release is fully drawn, and GDP growth in OECD economies contracts by 1.5–2.5 percentage points. A global technical recession (two consecutive quarters of negative growth) becomes the central expectation.

Scenario C (widening, 25 per cent probability) is the tail risk we are most concerned about. Should the conflict widen to include direct Saudi–Iranian hostilities or further destruction of Gulf production capacity, oil markets could enter a regime of genuine physical scarcity not seen since 1973. In this scenario, price becomes the rationing mechanism, and $200+ per barrel is not implausible. The macro consequences would be severe: deep recession, rapid de-anchoring of inflation expectations, and potential financial stability risks in energy-importing emerging markets (debt sustainability, currency crises, social instability).

12. Recommendations

Recommendation 1: Monetary Policy — Hold and Watch

Maintain the current policy rate. Signal willingness to "look through" first-round energy price effects, conditioned on medium-term inflation expectations remaining anchored. Prepare contingency plans for tightening if 5-year breakeven rates exceed [threshold] for more than four consecutive weeks.

Recommendation 2: Financial Stability Monitoring

Intensify monitoring of: (i) energy-sector credit exposures across the banking system; (ii) counterparty risk in commodity derivatives markets; (iii) sovereign debt sustainability in energy-importing emerging markets; (iv) insurance and reinsurance sector exposures to maritime losses in the Gulf. Activate enhanced reporting frequency (weekly rather than monthly) for these risk categories.

Recommendation 3: Coordination with Fiscal Authorities

Engage fiscal authorities to ensure that any consumer energy support measures (fuel subsidies, tax reductions) are temporary, targeted, and do not compromise the credibility of the inflation target. Advise against broad-based fuel subsidies that suppress the price signal needed for demand adjustment; favour targeted transfers to vulnerable households.

Recommendation 4: International Coordination

Engage with peer central banks (ECB, BOJ, BOE, RBI) on coordinated communication strategies to prevent disorderly market responses to the energy shock. The 2022 playbook — where major central banks tightened aggressively in response to energy-driven inflation — may not be appropriate for a shock of this structural severity. Coordinated dovish communication could prevent a simultaneous global policy-induced recession on top of the supply shock.

Recommendation 5: Scenario Preparedness

Develop operational plans for Scenario C (widening conflict, $150–200 oil, physical rationing). This includes: framework for emergency liquidity provision to energy-exposed financial institutions; coordination protocol with government on petroleum product allocation; stress-testing of national payment systems against fuel disruption scenarios; and contingency communication plans for financial market dislocation.
